#### What is AI

#### Simple explanation

## You've probably already used AI

If you've asked Copilot to help draft an email, used a tool to remove a background from an image, or had your voice transcribed automatically – you've used generative AI. You're not starting from scratch. This page is simply here to give you the words for what you're already doing.

## So what is generative AI, really?

Generative AI is software that creates new content based on what you ask it. When you give it an instruction (called a "prompt"), it generates a response – whether that's text, an image, a video clip, or audio.

The key word is "generates." Unlike traditional software that follows fixed rules, generative AI creates something new each time. Ask the same question twice and you might get slightly different answers. Request an image of a red barn and you'll get a unique image, not one pulled from a database.

This is what makes AI so powerful – and why we need to review its output carefully.

## The different types of generative AI

### Text (Large Language Models)

These are the tools most of us use daily: ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Claude, Gemini, Le Chat.

**How they work:** They've been trained on enormous amounts of text – books, websites, articles, conversations. When you type a prompt, the model predicts what words should come next based on patterns it learned during training. Think of it as a very well-read assistant that's excellent at producing relevant, coherent text.

**What you can use them for:**

- Drafting emails, reports, and communications
- Summarising long documents
- Rewriting text for different audiences
- Brainstorming ideas
- Translating between languages
- Explaining complex topics in simpler terms
- Analysing data and documents
- Writing and reviewing code

**Good to know:** These tools don't "know" things the way you do – they predict what words are likely to come next. That's why they can sound confident even when they're wrong.

### Images

Tools like DALL-E (built into ChatGPT), Midjourney, Adobe Firefly, and Stable Diffusion.

**How they work:** They've been trained on millions of images paired with descriptions. When you describe what you want, the model generates an image that matches your description. The image is created from scratch – it's not copied from anywhere.

**What you can use them for:**

- Creating visuals for presentations
- Generating concept images and mockups
- Editing and enhancing photos
- Removing or replacing backgrounds
- Creating variations of existing images
- Visualising ideas quickly

**Good to know:** AI-generated images can look very realistic, but they often have subtle errors – odd hands, inconsistent text, or strange details. Always review carefully, especially for professional use.

### Video

Tools like Runway, Pika, Sora, and various editing assistants.

**How they work:** Similar to image generation, but extended to moving images. Some tools generate video from text descriptions, others can edit existing footage, add effects, or extend clips.

**What you can use them for:**

- Creating short video clips for social media
- Editing and enhancing existing footage
- Adding effects and transitions
- Generating b-roll or background footage
- Creating animations from still images

**Good to know:** Video generation is newer and less mature than text or image AI. Quality varies significantly, and generating longer, coherent videos is still challenging.

### Audio

Tools like ElevenLabs, Murf, Descript, Suno, and transcription services.

**How they work:** Different tools handle different tasks. Voice generators create speech from text. Voice cloners replicate specific voices. Music generators create original compositions. Transcription tools convert speech to text.

**What you can use them for:**

- Generating voiceovers for videos or presentations
- Transcribing meetings and interviews
- Creating audio versions of written content
- Generating background music
- Editing podcasts and audio content

**Good to know:** Voice cloning raises significant ethical questions. Never clone someone's voice without their explicit permission.

## How is this different from a search engine?

A search engine finds existing content and shows you links. Generative AI creates new content on the spot, tailored to your request.

For example:

- **Google:** "best practices for product descriptions" → links to articles
- **ChatGPT:** "write a product description for organic fertiliser" → a draft you can use
- **Midjourney:** "photo of a modern barn at sunset" → a unique image that never existed before

This is what makes AI so useful – but also why we need to check its output. It doesn't retrieve facts; it generates plausible content.

## What AI is not

**AI is not a database of facts.**

It generates responses based on patterns, not by looking things up. It can be confidently wrong.

**AI is not connected to the internet by default.**

Most AI tools don't have live access to the web unless specifically enabled. Their knowledge has a cutoff date.

**AI is not a replacement for your expertise.**

It can help you work faster, but it can't make decisions for you – and it can't take responsibility for the result. That part is still on you.

**AI-generated content is not automatically usable.**

Whether it's text, images, or video – you need to review, edit, and verify before using it professionally.

## The bottom line

Generative AI is a set of tools that can create text, images, video, and audio based on your instructions. The technology is powerful and increasingly capable, but it's not magic – and it's not infallible.

Your job is to use these tools thoughtfully: give them clear instructions, review their output critically, and take responsibility for the final result.

That's what this Playbook will help you do.

#### How it works

# How AI Works

## A Look Under the Hood

You don’t need to be a developer to understand *how* AI generates responses. This page explains the core mechanics behind AI—why it works the way it does, and how you can use that knowledge to get better results.

---

## How Does AI Generate Responses?

### 1. **Training Data: The Foundation**

AI isn’t "thinking"—it’s **predicting** based on patterns in massive amounts of text (books, articles, websites, etc.).

- **Example**: If you ask AI to *"write a product description for organic fertilizer"*, it doesn’t pull from a database. Instead, it analyzes millions of similar texts to guess the most likely words to use.
- **Why it matters**: The quality of AI’s output depends on the data it was trained on. That’s why it excels at common tasks (like drafting emails) but may struggle with niche topics.

### 2. **Language Models: The Engine**

AI uses **language models** to process your input (called a *prompt*) and generate a response.

- **How it works**:
   - You type a prompt (e.g., *"Summarize this meeting note"*).
   - The AI breaks your prompt into *tokens* (words or phrases).
   - It predicts the most probable next words—like autocompleting a sentence, but on a massive scale.
- **Key insight**: AI doesn’t "understand" your request. It recognizes patterns and fills in the blanks.

### 3. **Probability: The Reason for Mistakes**

AI’s responses are based on **statistical probability**, not facts.

- **Why errors happen**:
   - If the training data contains biases or inaccuracies, the AI might repeat them.
   - It can’t verify facts—it only generates *plausible* text.
- **Your role**: Always review AI output for accuracy, especially for critical tasks.

---

## Why This Matters for You

- **Better prompts = better results**: Now that you know AI predicts responses, you can write prompts that guide it more effectively. Example:
   - ❌ *"Tell me about fertilizer."* (Too broad)
   - ✅ *"Write a 100-word product description for organic NPK fertilizer, aimed at Danish farmers. Highlight its sustainability benefits."* (Specific + structured)
- **Spot weaknesses**: If AI struggles with a task, it’s likely due to limited training data. Adjust your approach or verify the output.

---

## Quick Checklist: Using AI Effectively

✔ **Be specific**: Include context, audience, and goals in your prompt.

✔ **Review critically**: AI can sound confident even when wrong. Double-check facts.

✔ **Iterate**: If the first response isn’t perfect, refine your prompt and try again.

---

**Remember**: AI is a *tool*—not a replacement for your expertise. Use it to **augment** your work, not automate it entirely.

#### Common misconceptions

# Common misconceptions

## Separating myth from reality

There's a lot of confusion about what AI can and can't do. Some of it comes from hype, some from science fiction, and some from genuine misunderstanding of how these tools work.

This page addresses the most common misconceptions – so you can use AI with realistic expectations.

---

## "AI understands what it's saying"

**The reality:** AI doesn't understand anything. It processes patterns.

When ChatGPT writes a paragraph about climate change, it's not thinking about the topic. It's predicting which words are most likely to follow each other based on patterns it learned during training.

The same applies to image generators – they don't "see" the image they're creating. They're translating your text description into visual patterns.

**Why this matters:** AI can produce content that sounds (or looks) authoritative but is actually wrong. It has no concept of truth – only patterns.

---

## "AI is always right"

**The reality:** AI makes mistakes regularly.

Text AI can confidently state false information – a phenomenon called "hallucination." Image AI can generate people with six fingers. Video AI can produce physically impossible movements.

These tools are good at producing plausible content, not accurate content. There's a difference.

**Why this matters:** Never trust AI output without verification. Always review, fact-check, and apply your own expertise.

---

## "AI has access to the internet"

**The reality:** Most AI tools don't have live internet access by default.

ChatGPT, Claude, and similar tools have a knowledge cutoff date – they were trained on data up to a certain point and don't know what happened after. Some tools have optional web browsing features, but these need to be specifically enabled.

Image and video generators work entirely from their training data – they can't look up references online.

**Why this matters:** Don't ask AI about recent events, current prices, or live data without enabling web search. And even then, verify the information.

---

## "AI remembers everything I've told it"

**The reality:** Memory works differently across tools – and often not at all.

Most AI tools start fresh with each conversation. Claude, Gemini, and Le Chat don't remember previous conversations by default. ChatGPT has an optional memory feature, but it's limited.

Within a single conversation, AI can "forget" earlier context if the conversation gets too long (this is the context window limit).

**Why this matters:** Don't assume AI knows what you discussed yesterday. Provide context at the start of each conversation if needed.

---

## "AI-generated images are copied from real images"

**The reality:** AI generates new images based on learned patterns – it doesn't copy and paste.

When DALL-E creates an image, it's not pulling an existing image from a database. It's generating pixels based on patterns learned from its training data.

That said, AI can sometimes produce images that closely resemble training data, especially for famous artworks, logos, or well-known photographs.

**Why this matters:** AI images are original creations, but may still have copyright and ethical implications. Be especially careful with recognisable styles, trademarked elements, or images of real people.

---

## "AI can replace human expertise"

**The reality:** AI is a tool that enhances expertise – it doesn't replace it.

AI can help you draft faster, brainstorm more, and handle routine tasks. But it can't make judgment calls, understand context the way you do, or take responsibility for decisions.

A marketing professional using AI is still a marketing professional. The AI helps with execution; the expertise provides direction.

**Why this matters:** Use AI to amplify your skills, not to skip the parts that require actual thinking. The output is only as good as the human reviewing it.

---

## "AI will take my job"

**The reality:** AI changes how work gets done – but rarely replaces the whole job.

History shows that automation typically transforms jobs rather than eliminating them. Spreadsheets didn't eliminate accountants; they changed what accountants do.

The bigger risk isn't AI taking your job – it's someone who knows how to use AI effectively being more productive than someone who doesn't.

**Why this matters:** Learning to use AI well is a career skill. The people who thrive will be those who can combine AI capabilities with human judgment, creativity, and expertise.

---

## "Free AI tools are private"

**The reality:** Free often means your data is part of the product.

Many free AI tools use your conversations to train future models. Your prompts might be reviewed by humans at the company. Some tools store your data indefinitely.

Paid and enterprise versions typically offer stronger privacy protections – but you still need to check the settings.

**Why this matters:** Always configure your privacy settings before using AI for work. When in doubt, assume free tools are not private.

---

## "AI-generated content is always legal to use"

**The reality:** The legal landscape is complicated and evolving.

Copyright questions around AI are still being settled in courts around the world. Issues include: Can AI-generated content be copyrighted? Does AI training infringe on original creators' rights? Who owns what AI creates?

Additionally, some AI-generated content may inadvertently include trademarked elements or recognisable likenesses.

**Why this matters:** Don't assume AI output is automatically "free to use." Be especially careful with AI-generated images of real people, branded content, and content that resembles existing creative works.

---

## "More expensive AI is always better"

**The reality:** The best tool depends on the task.

GPT-4 isn't always better than GPT-3.5 – sometimes it's just slower and more expensive. Claude isn't always better than ChatGPT. The "best" tool depends on what you're doing.

For quick questions, a smaller, faster model might be perfect. For complex analysis, you might need the more powerful option.

**Why this matters:** Match the tool to the task. Don't pay for capabilities you don't need, and don't use a sledgehammer to hang a picture frame.

---

## "AI will get it right if I just ask again"

**The reality:** Repeating the same prompt usually gives similar results.

If AI gives you a poor response, asking the exact same question again rarely helps. You need to change your approach – provide more context, be more specific, or try a different angle.

Sometimes the problem is the prompt. Sometimes the AI simply isn't good at that particular task.

**Why this matters:** Learn to iterate on prompts rather than just retrying. If something isn't working after a few attempts, try a different tool or approach.

---

## The bottom line

AI is powerful but imperfect. It's a tool that can dramatically improve your productivity – if you understand its limitations.

The most effective AI users are those who:

- Verify output instead of trusting blindly
- Understand that "plausible" doesn't mean "correct"
- Match the right tool to the right task
- Keep learning as the technology evolves

That's not a weakness – it's just how tools work. A hammer is incredibly useful if you know when to use it. AI is the same.

Without context, AI gives generic answers. With context, it gives relevant ones. Custom instructions mean you don't have to explain who you are and what you need in every single conversation.

# ChatGPT Setup Guide

**Get ChatGPT configured for work in about 5 minutes.**

ChatGPT is the most widely used AI tool at Danish Agro. This guide walks you through the essential settings to make it work better for you — and keep your data safe.

## Quick setup checklist

- [ ] Turn off training data sharing
- [ ] Choose a Base Style and Tone
- [ ] Set up Custom Instructions
- [ ] Decide on Memory settings
- [ ] Test with a non-sensitive prompt

## Step 1: Turn off training

By default, OpenAI may use your conversations to train future models. Turn this off for work use.

1. Click your profile icon (bottom left)
2. Go to **Settings** → **Data Controls**
3. Turn OFF: **"Improve the model for everyone"**
4. Confirm your choice

**What this does:** Your conversations won't be used to train ChatGPT's models. They're still stored in your history, but OpenAI's trainers won't review them.

**Note:** ChatGPT Enterprise already has this disabled by default.

## Step 2: Choose your Base Style and Tone

ChatGPT offers preset "personalities" that change how it communicates. This is separate from Custom Instructions – it's a quick way to set a baseline tone.

1. Go to **Settings** → **Personalization**
2. Find **Base Style and Tone**
3. Choose from:

**Default**

Standard ChatGPT style: clear and neutral. Good for most tasks.

**Professional**

Polished and precise. Uses formal language and professional writing conventions. Good for workplace communication and documentation.

**Efficient**

Concise and direct. Gives you the answer first, then reasoning. Good for technical tasks, checklists, and when you want fast answers.

**Friendly**

Light and playful tone. Uses creative metaphors and makes serious topics feel lighter. Good for brainstorming and creative work.

And several others depending on your preferences.

**Good to know:**

- Your chosen style doesn't affect what ChatGPT can or can't do
- When you request specific content (emails, code, reports), ChatGPT matches the format to your request – not to the personality
- Your style works alongside Custom Instructions and Memory

**For work at Danish Agro:**

- **Default** or **Professional** for most tasks
- **Efficient** when you need quick, direct answers
- **Friendly** for brainstorming and creative work

## Step 3: Set up Custom Instructions

Custom Instructions tell ChatGPT who you are and how you want it to respond. This saves you from repeating the same context in every conversation.

1. Click your profile icon → **Settings** → **Personalization**
2. Enable **Custom Instructions**
3. Fill in two sections:

### Section 1: "What would you like ChatGPT to know about you?"

This is context about you. Example:

```other
I work in marketing at Danish Agro, an agricultural company.
I create content for dairy farmers and feed customers.
I need help with copywriting, campaign ideas, and content structure.
I prefer clear, direct language without corporate jargon.
```

### Section 2: "How would you like ChatGPT to respond?"

This is about how it should answer. Example:

```other
Be direct and concise. Skip the fluff.
Use bullet points for lists.
If you're not sure about something, say so.
Don't repeat my question back to me.
Suggest alternatives when relevant.
```

**Tip:** Keep instructions short and specific. You can always update them as you learn what works.

## Step 4: Configure Memory

ChatGPT has a Memory feature that remembers facts across all your conversations.

**To manage Memory:**

1. Go to **Settings** → **Personalization** → **Memory**
2. Choose to enable or disable it
3. Review stored memories and delete any you don't want kept

**Should you enable it?**

- ✅ **Yes:** For personal productivity, learning, non-work topics
- ⚠️ **Maybe:** For work, if you only share non-sensitive information
- ❌ **No:** If you often work with confidential data

**Pro tip:** Review your memories monthly. Delete anything you don't want stored long-term.

## Step 5: Use Temporary Chat for sensitive topics

If you need to discuss something sensitive without it being stored:

1. Click your profile → **Temporary Chat**
2. This conversation won't be saved or used for training
3. Memory is disabled for this session

**Use this when:**

- Testing ideas with potentially sensitive data (even anonymised)
- You don't want this conversation in your history
- You're discussing information you wouldn't want stored

## Managing your conversation history

### Archive conversations

- Hover over a conversation → Click **"..."** → **Archive**
- Access archived chats: Sidebar → **Archived**

### Delete conversations

- Hover over a conversation → Click **"..."** → **Delete**
- Delete all: **Settings → Data Controls → Delete all conversations**

### Search your history

- Use the search bar in the sidebar
- Search by topic, date, or keywords

**Pro tip:** Every 3-6 months, review and delete old work conversations.

## Understanding context limits

ChatGPT can handle roughly 8,000-32,000 words per conversation (depending on your version). That's about 1-2 research papers.

**When you hit the limit:**

- ChatGPT starts "forgetting" earlier parts of your conversation
- It might lose track of instructions from the beginning
- Answers become less relevant

**How to manage this:**

- Start new conversations for new topics
- Summarise context when continuing work: "Continuing from earlier: We discussed X, Y, Z. Now I need help with..."

## Conversation style

ChatGPT's default mode is conversational and balanced. You can adjust the output by asking:

- "Be more concise"
- "Explain like I'm a beginner"
- "Use more technical language"
- "Format this as a table"

## Common issues

## Step 5: Understanding conversation context limits

**Why this matters:** AI tools can't remember infinite amounts of text. They have a "context window" — think of it as short-term memory.

### What this means in practice

- **ChatGPT (GPT-4):** ~8,000-32,000 words (depending on version)
- **Claude:** ~200,000 words (~500 pages) — massive context window
- **Gemini:** ~30,000-1,000,000+ words (Gemini 1.5 Pro has the largest)
- **Le Chat:** ~32,000-128,000 words (depending on model selected)
- **M365 Copilot:** Varies by app, typically ~8,000-32,000 words

**Real-world comparison:**

- ChatGPT: ~1-2 research papers
- Claude: ~2-3 books
- Gemini Pro: ~5-10 books
- Le Chat (Large): ~1-2 books

**When you hit the limit:**

- The AI starts "forgetting" earlier parts of your conversation
- It might lose track of instructions you gave at the start
- Answers become less relevant

**How to manage this:**

1. **Start a new conversation** when:
   - You're switching topics completely
   - The AI seems to "forget" earlier context
   - The conversation is getting very long
2. **Summarize context** in a new chat:

```other
"Continuing from earlier: We're working on a customer presentation 
for dairy farmers about sustainable feed solutions. So far we've 
covered [X, Y, Z]. Now I need help with..."
```

3. **Use Custom Instructions/Styles** to avoid repeating basic context

**Tool selection based on context needs:**

- **Short tasks (< 5 pages):** Any tool works fine
- **Medium documents (5-50 pages):** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Le Chat
- **Large documents (50-500 pages):** Claude or Gemini Pro
- **Multiple large files:** Claude or Gemini with file upload

## Why bother with setup?

You could jump straight into AI without any preparation. Many people do. But spending 15 minutes on setup makes a real difference to your experience.

### Protect your data

By default, most AI tools use your conversations to train future versions of their models. That means the text you write, the questions you ask, and any data you include could be reviewed by the company's employees or influence how the AI responds to other users.

For personal use, that might be fine. For work, it's a problem. You don't want internal information, customer details, or business strategies becoming part of an AI's training data.

The good news: every major AI tool lets you turn this off. It takes about 30 seconds in the settings. But you have to know it's there, and you have to remember to do it.

### Get better responses

AI tools work better when they know who they're talking to. Without context, they give generic answers. With context, they give relevant ones.

Most tools let you set up "custom instructions" or a profile that tells the AI about your role, your preferences, and how you like to receive information. Instead of starting every conversation with "I work in marketing at an agricultural company and I need help with...", the AI already knows.

This isn't just convenient. It genuinely improves the quality of what you get back. The AI can tailor its language, examples, and level of detail to match your actual needs.

### Avoid embarrassing mistakes

AI is confident. It presents information as if it's certain, even when it's wrong. It invents sources, makes up statistics, and states opinions as facts. If you don't know what to look for, you might send that content to a customer or publish it on a website.

Understanding the Golden Rules and knowing how to check AI output helps you catch these problems before they become embarrassing. It's the difference between "AI helped me write this" and "AI made me look unprofessional."

### Build confidence

Many people feel uncertain about using AI at work. Is this allowed? Am I doing it right? What if I make a mistake?

That uncertainty is uncomfortable, and it holds people back from getting value from these tools. When you understand the basics, you stop second guessing yourself. You know what's safe to share, you know how to check output, and you know what to do if something goes wrong.

That confidence lets you focus on the actual work instead of worrying about whether you're breaking rules you don't fully understand.

## What you need to know about data security

You’re probably already using AI in your work – and that’s completely fine. But when we work with AI tools, we’re sending data outside Danish Agro’s systems. That’s why it’s important you know what you can share – and what you can’t.

This isn’t about stopping you. It’s about helping you use AI safely, so you can leverage the technology without putting the company or our customers in a vulnerable position.

## The basic rule: Think before you share

When you use an AI tool (ChatGPT, Copilot, or others), always assume that:

- Data can be stored by the tool
- Data can be used to train future models
- Data can be visible to others (depending on settings)
- You don’t have full control over where it ends up

**Therefore:** Never share anything you wouldn’t put on a public drive.

---

## ✅ What you can share

### General knowledge and open data

- Publicly available information
- Anonymized datasets
- General industry facts
- Open market analyses
- Published financial reports (after publication)

### Your own texts for improvement

- Texts you’ve written yourself (when they don’t contain sensitive data)
- Drafts for blog posts, press releases, or campaign texts
- Generic product descriptions
- Internal communication templates (without names, numbers, or details)

### Examples of everyday use

✅ “Rewrite this product description to make it more engaging”
✅ “Create a list of 10 headlines for a blog post about sustainability in agriculture”
✅ “Explain the difference between GDP and GNP”
✅ “Help me structure meeting minutes”

- Names of employees or customers

- Phone numbers
- Social security numbers
- Addresses
- Employment information
- Salary data
- Health information

### Customer data

- Customer contracts

### Technical security data

## Rule 1: Always review AI output

**AI is a powerful assistant, but you're the expert.**

### Why this matters

AI can produce impressive results quickly — but it can also make mistakes, invent facts (hallucinations), or miss important nuances.

Your job isn't just to use AI. It's to ensure the final result is accurate, appropriate and professionally sound.

### What this means in practice

AI should help you work better, not think less.

**✅ Good uses of AI (enhancing your work):**

Check facts — Verify any statistics, dates, names or claims

Read critically — Does it actually answer the question? Is the logic sound?

Match your voice — Does it sound like you/Danish Agro, or is it generic?

Consider context — Is it appropriate for the audience and situation?

Ask yourself — "Would I be comfortable signing my name to this?"

**❌ Bad uses of AI (replacing your thinking):**

Copy-pasting AI output without reading it carefully

#### Rule 2: Never share confidential data

## Rule 2: Never share confidential data

**AI tools can't always protect sensitive information — you need to.**

### Why this matters

Most AI tools (including ChatGPT, Copilot free tier, and others) process your inputs on external servers. Even "approved" tools have limitations on what data they can safely handle.

Once you share confidential information with an AI tool, you can't control where it goes or who might see it. That's why you need to be the first line of defense for sensitive data.

### What this means in practice

**Think before you paste or type:**

✅ **You CAN share:**

- Public information (already on our website, press releases)
- Anonymized data (no names, no personal details, no identifying information)
- General industry knowledge and best practices
- Your own ideas and draft concepts
- Published research and publicly available statistics

❌ **You CAN'T share:**

- Customer names, contact details, or any personal information
- Financial data (budgets, revenue, forecasts, pricing)
- Unpublished strategy or business plans
- Employee personal data (salaries, performance reviews, private info)
- Confidential contracts or agreements
- Internal communications not meant for public
- Anything marked "confidential" or "internal only"

⚠️ **Gray zone — when in doubt:**

- Data that might be public but you're not 100% sure
- Information about ongoing projects not yet announced
- Technical details that could be sensitive
- Anything you'd hesitate to post on LinkedIn

**If you're uncertain: Don't share it. Ask first.**

### Real example

**Situation:** You're working on a customer presentation and want AI to help structure the content.

**❌ DON'T do this:**

> "Help me create a presentation for Company A about our new pricing model. We're offering them 15% discount on bulk orders over 500 tons, which is better than the 10% we give Company B.”

**Why it's wrong:**

- Customer names (Company A, Company B)
- Specific pricing (15%, 10%)
- Commercial terms (discount structure)
- Competitive information

**✅ DO this instead:**

> "Help me create a presentation structure for a large dairy customer about our bulk order pricing model. Focus on: volume benefits, quality assurance, delivery flexibility, and partnership value."

**What changed:**

- Generic description ("large dairy customer" not “Company A”)
- No specific numbers or terms
- Focus on structure and concepts, not confidential details
- You can add the real data manually later

### Quick checklist

Before sharing anything with AI, ask yourself:

- [ ] Is this information already public?
- [ ] Would I be comfortable posting this on social media?
- [ ] Does this contain anyone's personal information?
- [ ] Does this include financial or commercial data?
- [ ] Am I revealing strategy or plans not yet announced?
- [ ] Could this harm Danish Agro or a customer if it leaked?

**If you answered "no" to question 1 or "yes" to questions 3-6: Don't share it.**

### When you're not sure

If you're uncertain whether something is confidential:

- **Remove it** — better safe than sorry
- **Anonymize first** — remove names, numbers, identifying details
- **Ask your manager** or the Data Protection Officer
- **Use placeholders** — [customer name], [amount], [date]
- **Add details manually later** after AI gives you the structure

**Remember:** It takes 2 seconds to ask, but data breaches can take years to fix.

#### Rule 3: Be transparent when you use AI

## Rule 3: Be transparent when you use AI

**Honesty builds trust — with customers, colleagues and yourself.**

### Why this matters

There's nothing wrong with using AI to help with your work. But people deserve to know when they're reading AI-assisted content, especially customers and stakeholders.

Transparency isn't about apologizing for using AI. It's about being professional and maintaining trust. It shows you're using AI responsibly, not trying to deceive anyone.

### What this means in practice

**When you should disclose AI use:**

✅ **Always disclose when:**

- Sending AI-generated content to customers or external partners
- Publishing content on our website, social media, or marketing materials
- Sharing reports or analysis with leadership
- Creating official documents or communications
- Using AI for customer service responses

✅ **Consider disclosing when:**

- Collaborating with colleagues on projects
- Presenting work in meetings
- Sharing drafts for feedback

❌ **You don't need to disclose when:**

- Using AI for personal brainstorming or idea generation
- Getting help with grammar/spell checking
- Using AI as a research assistant (but verify facts!)
- Asking AI for suggestions you completely rewrite

**The key question:** Will someone else see or be affected by this content? If yes, be transparent.

#### Rule 4: Use AI to enhance, not replace, your expertise

## Rule 4: Use AI to enhance, not replace, your expertise

**You're the expert. AI is the assistant.**

### Why this matters

AI is incredibly good at generating content quickly — but it doesn't understand your business, your customers, or your strategic goals the way you do.

If you simply copy-paste AI output, you're not adding your expertise, judgment, or insight. The result is generic work that could have been done by anyone. Your value isn't in using AI — it's in using AI *plus* your professional experience.

### What this means in practice

**AI should help you work better, not think less.**

✅ **Good uses of AI (enhancing your work):**

- **Overcoming blank page syndrome** — AI drafts a structure, you add substance
- **Speeding up research** — AI summarizes information, you verify and interpret
- **Generating multiple options** — AI creates variations, you choose the best
- **Improving clarity** — AI suggests simpler wording, you decide if it fits
- **Handling routine tasks** — AI drafts the email, you personalize it

❌ **Bad uses of AI (replacing your thinking):**

- Copy-pasting AI output without reading it carefully
- Letting AI make strategic decisions for you
- Using AI's first draft as the final version
- Accepting AI's suggestions without questioning them
- Relying on AI for expertise you're supposed to provide

**The test:** If someone asks "Why did you choose this approach?" can you explain it in your own words?

### Real example

**Situation:** You need to create a marketing campaign for a new product line.

**❌ BAD approach (AI replaces you):**

1. "Create a complete marketing campaign for agricultural equipment"
2. Copy the entire AI response
3. Send it to your manager without changes

**Result:** Generic campaign that doesn't reflect Danish Agro's brand, customer insights, or market position.

---

**✅ GOOD approach (AI enhances you):**

**Step 1 - Use AI for structure:**

> "What are the key components of a B2B marketing campaign? Give me a framework."

**Step 2 - Add your expertise:**

- You fill in Danish Agro's specific value propositions
- You add customer insights from recent conversations
- You adjust tone to match your audience
- You incorporate seasonal timing (harvest cycles)

**Step 3 - Use AI for refinement:**

> "Review this campaign outline [paste your version] and suggest improvements for clarity and impact"

**Step 4 - Final judgment:**

- You evaluate AI's suggestions
- You keep what works, ignore what doesn't
- You make the final call based on your experience

**Result:** A campaign that combines AI's efficiency with your strategic thinking and market knowledge.

# Configuring Your AI Tool

**Get better results by setting up your AI tool properly from the start.**

Most people jump straight into using ChatGPT or Copilot without adjusting any settings. That's like using a new phone without setting it up — it works, but you're missing out on features that make it better for *you*.

This guide shows you the essential settings you should configure before you start working.

---

## Step 1: Turn off data sharing for training

**Why this matters:** By default, many AI tools use your conversations to improve their models. That means your inputs could be seen by the company's trainers or even influence future responses to other users.

### ChatGPT (Free, Plus, Team)

1. Click your profile icon (bottom left)
2. Go to **Settings** → **Data Controls**
3. Turn OFF: **"Improve the model for everyone"**
4. Confirm your choice

**What this does:** Your conversations won't be used to train ChatGPT's models. They're still stored in your history, but OpenAI's trainers won't review them.

**Note:** ChatGPT Enterprise and Danish Agro's M365 Copilot already have this disabled by default.

### Microsoft Copilot (Free version)

The free Copilot doesn't offer an option to disable training. This is why we recommend using:

- **M365 Copilot** (paid, integrated with your Danish Agro account) for work
- **ChatGPT** (with training disabled) as an alternative for non-sensitive work

### Claude (by Anthropic)

1. Click your profile icon (bottom right)
2. Go to **Settings** → **Privacy**
3. Turn OFF: **"Allow Anthropic to use your conversations to train our models"**
4. Confirm your choice

**What this does:** Your conversations won't be used to train Claude's models. Anthropic also offers Enterprise plans with enhanced data protection.

**Note:** Claude is particularly good for:

- Long documents (huge context window ~200k tokens)
- Complex analysis and reasoning
- Code review and debugging
- Working with multiple files simultaneously

### Google Gemini

1. Click the menu icon (three lines, top left)
2. Go to **Settings** → **Privacy**
3. Under **Gemini Apps Activity**:
   - Turn OFF: **"Save Gemini Apps Activity"** (stops saving to your Google Account)
   - Or use **Auto-delete** to automatically remove after 3, 18, or 36 months
4. Review **Apps & services** settings

**What this does:** Prevents Google from storing your Gemini conversations in your Google Account activity. However, Google may still temporarily process your prompts.

**Note:** Gemini integrates with Google Workspace (Docs, Gmail, etc.), which can be powerful but requires careful data handling.

### Le Chat (by Mistral)

1. Click your profile icon (top right)
2. Go to **Settings** → **Privacy**
3. Turn OFF: **"Contribute to model improvement"**
4. Confirm your choice

**What this does:** Your conversations won't be used to improve Mistral's models. Le Chat is a European AI (based in France), which some users prefer for data residency reasons.

**Note:** Le Chat is particularly good for:

- Multilingual content (strong European language support)
- Fast responses (lightweight models available)
- Users who prefer EU-based AI providers
- Code generation and technical tasks

### Other AI tools

Check each tool's privacy settings. If you can't find a clear opt-out for training:

- Assume your data might be used
- Only use it for non-sensitive, public information
- Consider sticking to approved tools for work

**Pro tip:** Check this setting regularly. Sometimes tools update their policies and reset your preferences.

## Step 3: Understand and manage memory

**Why this matters:** Some AI tools remember information across conversations. This can be helpful (it knows your preferences) or risky (it stores sensitive info you shared once).

### ChatGPT Memory

ChatGPT has a **Memory** feature (if enabled):

- It remembers facts you've told it across *all* conversations
- Example: "I work at Danish Agro" → it remembers for next time
- You can review and delete memories: **Settings → Personalization → Memory**

**Should you enable it?**

- ✅ YES: For personal productivity, learning, non-work stuff
- ⚠️ MAYBE: For work, if you only share non-sensitive information
- ❌ NO: If you often work with confidential data

**Pro tip:** Review your memory monthly. Delete anything you don't want stored long-term.

### Claude Memory

Claude currently doesn't have a persistent memory feature across conversations. Each conversation is independent.

**What this means:**

- You need to provide context at the start of each new conversation
- Use Custom Styles (see Step 2) to save your preferences
- Or start with a context-setting message: "I'm [role] working on [topic]..."

**Advantage:** More privacy — nothing carries over between conversations automatically.

### Gemini Memory

Gemini saves conversation history in your Google Account activity (unless you disabled it in Step 1).

**Gemini doesn't have explicit "memory" across chats, but:**

- It can access your Google Workspace data (if you grant permission)
- Activity history affects what Google "knows" about you
- Use **Gems** to save personalized assistant configurations

### Le Chat Memory

Le Chat doesn't have persistent memory across conversations by default.

**What this means:**

- Each conversation starts fresh
- Use **Agents** (see Step 2) to save your preferences and context
- You can reference previous messages within the same conversation

**Advantage:** Clean slate for each session — no accidental data carryover.

## Step 5: Understanding conversation context limits

**Why this matters:** AI tools can't remember infinite amounts of text. They have a "context window" — think of it as short-term memory.

### What this means in practice

- **ChatGPT (GPT-4):** ~8,000-32,000 words (depending on version)
- **Claude:** ~200,000 words (~500 pages) — massive context window
- **Gemini:** ~30,000-1,000,000+ words (Gemini 1.5 Pro has the largest)
- **Le Chat:** ~32,000-128,000 words (depending on model selected)
- **M365 Copilot:** Varies by app, typically ~8,000-32,000 words

**Real-world comparison:**

- ChatGPT: ~1-2 research papers
- Claude: ~2-3 books
- Gemini Pro: ~5-10 books
- Le Chat (Large): ~1-2 books

**When you hit the limit:**

- The AI starts "forgetting" earlier parts of your conversation
- It might lose track of instructions you gave at the start
- Answers become less relevant

**How to manage this:**

1. **Start a new conversation** when:
   - You're switching topics completely
   - The AI seems to "forget" earlier context
   - The conversation is getting very long
2. **Summarize context** in a new chat:

```other
"Continuing from earlier: We're working on a customer presentation 
for dairy farmers about sustainable feed solutions. So far we've 
covered [X, Y, Z]. Now I need help with..."
```

3. **Use Custom Instructions/Styles** to avoid repeating basic context

**Tool selection based on context needs:**

- **Short tasks (< 5 pages):** Any tool works fine
- **Medium documents (5-50 pages):** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Le Chat
- **Large documents (50-500 pages):** Claude or Gemini Pro
- **Multiple large files:** Claude or Gemini with file upload

# Quality Checklist

## Check your work before it goes out

AI can produce impressive content fast — but it can also confidently present mistakes as facts. This checklist helps you catch problems before they reach your colleagues or customers.

---

## Why this matters

71% of Danish Agro employees say their biggest concern with AI is the quality of the output. That's a good instinct. AI doesn't know when it's wrong, so the responsibility for quality sits with you.

The good news: most issues are easy to spot if you know what to look for. This 2-minute checklist catches the most common problems.

## Before you use AI output

Run through these checks — it takes 2 minutes and catches most problems.

### ✅ Accuracy

- [ ] **I've read the entire output** (not just skimmed it)
- [ ] **Facts and numbers are verified** (or flagged for checking)
- [ ] **No made-up sources or references** (AI loves inventing these)
- [ ] **Names, dates, and specifics are correct**

### ✅ Appropriateness

- [ ] **Tone matches the audience** (not too formal, not too casual)
- [ ] **No confidential information included** (even if I put it in the prompt)
- [ ] **Follows brand voice** (sounds like Danish Agro, not generic AI)
- [ ] **No bias or stereotypes** (check assumptions about groups)

### ✅ Completeness

- [ ] **Answers the actual question/task** (not a related but different one)
- [ ] **Nothing important is missing** (AI sometimes skips key points)
- [ ] **Length is appropriate** (not padded, not cut short)

### ✅ Readiness

- [ ] **I've edited it** (not just copy-pasted)
- [ ] **It's in my voice** (I'd be comfortable if someone asked "did you write this?")
- [ ] **Grammar and spelling checked** (AI makes mistakes too)

---

## Red flags to watch for

If you spot any of these, stop and verify before using:

🚩 **Suspiciously specific statistics** – "Studies show 73.2% of customers prefer..." (probably made up)

🚩 **Named sources you can't verify** – Books, articles, or quotes that don't exist

🚩 **Confident claims about recent events** – AI knowledge has a cutoff date

🚩 **Perfect-sounding but vague content** – Reads well but says nothing specific

🚩 **Repetitive phrases or structure** – Same pattern repeated throughout

🚩 **Unusual words or phrasing** – AI has favorites ("delve", "landscape", "leverage")

---

## When to do extra checks

Some content needs more careful review. Add extra verification time for:

**High-stakes content** Anything going to customers, press, or senior management

**Technical or specialist content** AI can sound authoritative about things it doesn't understand

**Legal or compliance-related content** Never trust AI for legal accuracy

**Content with specific numbers** Prices, dates, measurements, statistics – verify all of them

**Content about real people or companies** AI can mix up facts or invent details

## Quick copy: The 4-question test

If you only have 30 seconds, ask yourself:

1. **Did I read it?** – Actually read, not skimmed
2. **Is it true?** – Facts I can stand behind
3. **Is it appropriate?** – Right tone, no sensitive data
4. **Is it mine?** – I've edited it, I own it

#### How to Validate AI Output

#### Layer 1: Fact-checking methods

### Cross-reference specific claims

**What to check:**

- Statistics and percentages
- Dates and timelines
- Names of people, companies, products
- Technical specifications
- Prices or financial figures
- Legal or regulatory requirements

**How to check:**

**Method 1: Google it directly**

Copy the specific claim into Google. If AI says "Studies show 67% of farmers prefer...", search for that exact statistic.

**Method 2: Check original sources**

If AI mentions a study, report, or article, verify it exists. AI frequently invents source titles that sound real but don't exist.

**Method 3: Verify with internal systems**

For company-specific information (prices, product specs, policies), always check against your own systems. Never trust AI for internal data.

**Method 4: Ask a subject expert**

When in doubt, ask someone who knows. A 30-second Slack message can save hours of fixing mistakes later.

**Red flags:**

- Oddly specific statistics (e.g., "73.2% of customers...")
- Round numbers that should vary (e.g., "exactly 50% increase")
- Recent events or data (AI knowledge has a cutoff date)
- Technical details that sound impressive but vague

---

### The "too good to check" trap

Sometimes AI produces content that's so well-written, so confident, and so aligned with what you wanted to hear that you're tempted to skip verification.

**Don't.**

The better it sounds, the more important it is to verify. AI is particularly good at producing plausible-sounding nonsense in areas where it has limited actual knowledge.

**Example:**

AI might confidently explain Danish Agro's market share in the Baltic states, complete with specific percentages and growth trends — even if you never shared that data and it doesn't have access to it.

**Solution:**

If content contains specific facts you didn't provide and can't immediately verify, either check them or remove them.

---

### Checking sources and citations

**The problem:** AI loves to cite sources. Unfortunately, it also loves to invent them.

**Common patterns:**

**Made-up sources:**

- Journal articles with plausible titles that don't exist
- Books by real authors that were never written
- Reports from real organizations that were never published
- "According to [Source]" where [Source] is completely fictional

**Misattributed quotes:**

- Real quotes assigned to wrong people
- Paraphrased content presented as direct quotes
- Quotes that capture the spirit of someone's work but were never actually said

**Outdated or deprecated sources:**

- Citations to older versions of documents
- References to policies that have changed
- Studies that have been superseded by newer research

**How to verify sources:**

**Step 1: Search for the exact title**

Google the full title in quotation marks. If it doesn't appear, it probably doesn't exist.

**Step 2: Check the author**

Does this author actually write about this topic? Do they work in this field?

**Step 3: Verify publication venue**

Does the journal, website, or publisher actually exist? Do they cover this topic area?

**Step 4: Use Google Scholar**

For academic sources, search Google Scholar. Real research papers will show up there.

**Step 5: When in doubt, remove**

If you can't verify a source in 2 minutes, either remove the citation or replace it with a source you can verify.

**Pro tip:**

AI sometimes produces real sources but gets the details wrong (wrong year, wrong author, wrong title). If something seems off, verify the full citation.

#### Layer 2: Tone & style review

### Does it sound right for the situation?

**Check for:**

**Formality level**

- Too formal for the situation? (e.g., "We hereby inform you..." in a casual email)
- Too casual for the context? (e.g., "Hey!" in a formal proposal)

**Audience appropriateness**

- Is the language level right? (Not too technical for general audience, not too simple for experts)
- Are examples and references relevant to your actual audience?

**Brand voice alignment**

- Does it sound like Danish Agro? (or is it generic corporate speak?)
- Would your colleagues recognize this as your work?

**Cultural and contextual fit**

- Are idioms and references appropriate for Danish/Nordic context?
- Does it account for local business practices?

**How to check:**

**Method 1: Read it out loud**

If it sounds unnatural when spoken, it needs editing.

**Method 2: Compare to real examples**

Pull up similar content you've written before. How does the tone compare?

**Method 3: The colleague test**

Would a colleague reading this think "yes, that's exactly how [your name] writes"?

**Method 4: Check for AI tells**

Certain phrases are AI favorites: "delve into," "landscape," "leverage," "robust," "streamline," "at the end of the day." Not wrong, but overused by AI.

---

### Removing generic AI language

**Common AI patterns to watch for:**

**Unnecessary qualifiers:**

- "It's important to note that..."
- "It's worth mentioning that..."
- "One should consider..."

**Overly formal transitions:**

- "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Nevertheless," used excessively
- Every paragraph starting with "Additionally,"

**Hedging language:**

- "May," "might," "could potentially" used too often
- Sounds uncertain even when being definitive is better

**Empty corporate speak:**

- "Synergy," "paradigm shift," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge"
- Phrases that sound important but say nothing

**How to fix:**

Edit for directness. Replace "It's important to note that prices vary by region" with "Prices vary by region."

---

#### Layer 3: Logic & reasoning checks

### Does the argument actually make sense?

AI can produce text that flows beautifully but contains logical flaws, contradictions, or reasoning that falls apart under scrutiny.

**Check for:**

**Internal contradictions**

- Does paragraph 3 contradict paragraph 1?
- Are recommendations consistent with the analysis?
- Do the examples support the main points?

**Cause and effect errors**

- Are claimed relationships actually causal or just correlational?
- Does the reasoning chain hold up? (If A, then B, therefore C)

**Missing steps**

- Are there gaps in the logic?
- Does it jump to conclusions without supporting evidence?

**Oversimplification**

- Does it present complex issues as too simple?
- Are nuances and exceptions ignored?

**How to check:**

**Method 1: The "therefore" test**

Read the content and add "therefore" between claims. Does the logic hold?

Example:

"Farmers are adopting precision agriculture. [Therefore?] Yields will increase 30%."

Missing step: Under what conditions? What about farmers who can't afford the tech?

**Method 2: The skeptic's read**

Pretend you disagree with the conclusion. Can you easily poke holes in the argument?

**Method 3: Check the math**

If there are calculations, percentages, or comparisons, verify them. AI frequently gets math wrong.

---

### Spotting false confidence

**The problem:** AI states everything with equal certainty, even when it's speculating or extrapolating.

**Watch for:**

**Definitive language about uncertain things**

- "This will result in..." (when it's actually "this might result in...")
- "Customers prefer..." (when there's no data cited)
- "The best approach is..." (when there are multiple valid approaches)

**Predictions presented as facts**

- "In 2025, the market will..." (it might, but AI doesn't know)
- "This strategy will increase revenue by..." (depends on many factors)

**Universal claims**

- "All farmers..." (there are always exceptions)
- "Everyone agrees..." (they definitely don't)

**How to fix:**

Add appropriate qualifiers. Change "This will increase efficiency" to "This can increase efficiency when implemented correctly."

---

#### Layer 4: Authenticity & ownership

### Making it yours

Even if everything is factually correct, appropriate, and logical — if it doesn't sound like you, it's not ready to use.

**The ownership test:**

**Question 1: Would I say this in conversation?**

If not, edit until you would.

**Question 2: If someone challenged this content, could I defend it?**

If you don't fully understand what you're sharing, you're not ready to own it.

**Question 3: Does this reflect my actual expertise?**

AI can make you sound like an expert on things you don't know. That's dangerous.

**How to personalize:**

**Add specific examples**

Replace AI's generic examples with real ones from your work.

**Include your perspective**

Add a sentence or two of your own thinking. What's your take?

**Use your voice**

If you normally use contractions, add them. If you prefer short sentences, break up long ones.

**Add context AI doesn't have**

Reference specific projects, colleagues (first names only), or internal context.

## Practical techniques for checking AI-generated content

AI doesn't know when it's wrong. It presents hallucinations with the same confidence as facts, mixes up dates, invents sources, and occasionally produces complete nonsense that sounds perfectly plausible.

This page gives you practical methods to catch these problems before they become embarrassing.

---

## Why validation matters

**The confidence problem:** AI never says "I'm not sure." It states everything with equal certainty, whether it's correct or completely made up.

**The quality gap:** AI can produce a first draft in 30 seconds. If you don't spend at least 2 minutes checking it, you're essentially trusting a tool that has no concept of truth to speak on your behalf.

**The responsibility shift:** When you use AI output, you own it. "The AI made a mistake" is not an excuse your customer, colleague, or manager will accept.

---

## The validation framework

Good validation has four layers. You don't always need all four, but knowing which to use when makes the difference.

### Layer 1: Accuracy (Facts & Numbers)

Check if the information is true

### Layer 2: Appropriateness (Tone & Context)

Check if it fits the situation

### Layer 3: Logic (Reasoning & Structure)

Check if the arguments make sense

### Layer 4: Authenticity (Voice & Ownership)

Check if it sounds like you

---

## The 5-minute deep check

When you have time for a thorough review (high-stakes content, customer-facing, or technically complex), use this process:

**Minute 1: Scan for red flags**

Skim the entire output looking for:

- Made-up statistics
- Invented sources
- Claims that seem suspiciously specific
- AI-favorite words and phrases

**Minute 2: Fact-check specifics**

Pick the 3-5 most important factual claims and verify them. If you find problems here, the whole output needs scrutiny.

**Minute 3: Read for logic**

Read straight through checking:

- Does each paragraph follow from the previous?
- Are there gaps or jumps in reasoning?
- Do examples actually support the points?

**Minute 4: Tone and voice check**

Read it out loud. Mark anything that doesn't sound like something you'd say.

**Minute 5: Edit and personalize**

Make it yours. Add your examples, adjust the tone, remove generic language.

**Result:** Content you can confidently put your name on.

---

---

## Common validation mistakes

**Mistake 1: Checking only the beginning**

AI often hides mistakes in the middle or end of long outputs. Read the whole thing.

**Mistake 2: Trusting because it "sounds smart"**

Sophisticated language ≠ accurate content. The fancier it sounds, the more you should verify.

**Mistake 3: Skipping verification on "small" content**

A mistake in a short email can be just as damaging as one in a long report.

**Mistake 4: Not checking your own edits**

After editing AI output, re-read it. Sometimes edits create new problems.

**Mistake 5: Assuming AI knows your context**

AI doesn't know company policies, internal processes, or current projects unless you explicitly told it. Don't assume it got these right.

---

## When to get a second opinion

Some content is too important or too risky to validate alone. Get a colleague to review when:

**High stakes:**

- Content going to customers, partners, or press
- Legal or compliance-related content
- Anything that could affect Danish Agro's reputation

**Outside your expertise:**

- Technical content you're not fully qualified to verify
- Topics where you're not the subject expert
- Industry-specific content in unfamiliar areas

**Sensitive topics:**

- Anything involving people (hiring, performance, complaints)
- Financial or pricing information
- Strategic or confidential matters

**When you're uncertain:**

- Something feels off but you can't pinpoint why
- Claims you can't easily verify
- Content that contradicts your understanding

**How to ask:**

"I used AI to draft this [type of content]. Could you quickly check if [specific aspect] looks right to you?"

---

## Building a validation habit

**Make it routine:**

**Every AI output gets at least 2 minutes** of review before use. No exceptions.

**Keep a checklist handy** (bookmark the Quality Checklist page) and run through it for important content.

**Learn from mistakes.** When you catch an error, note what type it was. You'll get better at spotting patterns.

**Share catches with colleagues.** When you find an AI mistake, tell your team. Everyone learns, and it reduces stigma around admitting AI got something wrong.

**Track your validation time.** You'll notice you get faster as you build the habit and learn what to look for.

---

## Remember

Good validation isn't about being paranoid. It's about being professional.

**The goal:** Use AI to work faster without sacrificing quality or accuracy.

**The method:** Quick checks for routine content, deeper review for important content.

**The mindset:** Trust but verify. AI is a powerful tool, but you're the one responsible for what goes out under your name.

#### Common AI Mistakes

#### Mistake Type 1: Hallucinations

### What it looks like

AI confidently states "facts" that are completely invented. It creates:

- Statistics that don't exist
- Sources that were never published
- People who don't exist (or gives real people fake credentials)
- Events that never happened
- Companies or products that aren't real

**The scary part:** Hallucinations look exactly like real information. There's no warning sign in the output itself.

### Why it happens

AI generates text by predicting what word comes next based on patterns in its training data. It doesn't "know" things - it recognizes patterns. When it encounters a gap in its knowledge, it fills in with plausible-sounding text rather than admitting uncertainty.

Think of it like autocomplete on steroids: it's trying to complete the sentence in the most likely way, not in the most truthful way.

### Common hallucination patterns

**Made-up statistics:**

- "Studies show 73.2% of Danish farmers prefer organic feed"
- "Market research indicates a 47% increase in precision agriculture adoption"
- "According to recent surveys, 8 out of 10 dairy producers..."

**Red flag:** Oddly specific numbers without named sources. Real statistics usually come with "According to [specific organization]" or "A 2024 study by [researcher] found..."

**Invented sources:**

- "As reported in the Journal of Agricultural Economics (2023)..."
- "According to the Danish Agro Industry Report..."
- "Smith & Johnson's research on sustainable farming..."

**Red flag:** Citations that sound academic but can't be found via Google Scholar or the organization's website.

**Fake quotes:**

- Real person, made-up quote: "As Peter Larsen from Danish Agro said, 'Innovation starts with trust.'"
- Fake expert: "According to Dr. Hansen, a leading expert in agricultural AI..."

**Red flag:** Quotes from internal people you can verify with, or "experts" with generic names you can't find online.

**Confident claims about company-specific information:**

- "Danish Agro's market share in Sweden grew by 15% last quarter"
- "The company's new product line generated €2M in revenue"
- "Customer satisfaction scores improved to 4.8/5"

**Red flag:** Internal data you never provided. If you didn't give AI the information and it's not public, it's probably invented.

### How to spot hallucinations fast

**The 30-second check:**

**Step 1:** Look for specific numbers, names, and sources

**Step 2:** Pick the most important one

**Step 3:** Google it in quotes

**Step 4:** If you can't verify it in 30 seconds, flag it or remove it

**The Google test:**

Copy suspicious claims into Google with quotation marks. If nothing comes up, it's likely invented.

**The "too convenient" test:**

If AI gives you exactly the statistic or source you needed to prove your point, be extra suspicious. Real research rarely lines up that perfectly.

**The internal knowledge test:**

For company-specific claims, ask yourself: "Did I tell AI this, or is it public information?" If neither, it's made up.

### Real examples from Danish Agro

**Hallucination:**

"Danish Agro's customer portal sees 15,000 daily active users with an average session time of 8.5 minutes."

**Why it's wrong:**

These specific metrics weren't provided to AI and aren't publicly available. AI invented plausible-sounding numbers.

**Hallucination:**

"According to the 2024 Nordic Agriculture Technology Report, Danish Agro ranks as the 3rd most innovative agri-business in the region."

**Why it's wrong:**

The "Nordic Agriculture Technology Report" doesn't exist. AI created a realistic-sounding source to support the claim.

### What to do when you find hallucinations

**Don't panic.** Hallucinations are normal AI behavior, not a sign you're using it wrong.

**Option 1: Verify and correct**

If the claim is useful, find the real source or data and replace the hallucination with accurate information.

**Option 2: Remove it**

If you can't quickly verify it, just delete the claim. Generic statements are better than invented facts.

**Option 3: Rephrase more generally**

Change "73% of farmers prefer organic feed" to "Many farmers are choosing organic feed options."

**Never:** Just assume it's probably correct because it sounds good.

#### Mistake Type 2: Outdated Information

### What it looks like

AI confidently describes situations, facts, or people as they were during its training period, not as they are now.

Examples:

- Describing someone in a job they left two years ago
- Referencing products that have been discontinued
- Citing policies that have changed
- Using old prices or specifications
- Referring to "recent" events that are actually years old

### Why it happens

AI's knowledge has a cutoff date (usually 1-2 years before the current date). It doesn't know about:

- Personnel changes
- Product launches or discontinuations
- Policy updates
- Market changes
- Recent news or events
- Current prices or availability

Think of it as talking to someone who's been out of the country for two years. They know a lot, but their knowledge stopped at a specific point.

### Common outdated information patterns

**Job titles and roles:**

- "Contact Peter Jensen, Head of Marketing at Danish Agro"

   (Peter moved to a different role last year)

**Product information:**

- "Danish Agro's FeedMaster 3000 is our flagship product"

   (FeedMaster 4000 launched 6 months ago)

**Pricing and availability:**

- "The standard membership fee is €199/year"

   (Pricing changed in the last price adjustment)

**Organizational structure:**

- "The IT department reports to the CFO"

   (Organizational restructuring changed this)

**"Recent" events that aren't recent:**

- "In recent months, the agriculture sector has..."

   (Describing something from 2023 as "recent" in 2026)

### How to spot outdated information fast

**Red flags:**

**Any names with titles or roles**

Always verify current roles for real people mentioned.

**"Recent," "currently," "now"**

When AI says something is recent or current, ask: relative to when?

**Specific prices, dates, or versions**

These change frequently. Verify before using.

**Descriptions of "the current situation"**

The current situation for AI might be 2 years old.

### The cutoff date rule

**Know your AI's knowledge cutoff:**

- GPT-4: April 2023
- Claude: Early 2023
- Copilot: Varies (but usually 1-2 years behind)

**If it matters when the information is from, verify it.**

Simple rule: Anything that could have changed in the last 2 years needs verification.

### What to do about outdated information

**For people and roles:**

Verify with your internal directory or ask someone who knows.

**For products and services:**

Check your own website or product catalog.

**For policies and procedures:**

Consult official documentation or your manager.

**For external information:**

Use web search to find current information.

**When in doubt:**

Remove specific claims and use more general language: "Contact your HR representative" instead of "Contact Anna Jensen in HR."

#### Mistake Type 3: Bias and Assumptions

### What it looks like

AI makes assumptions about gender, culture, geography, and roles that may not match your context or that reinforce stereotypes.

Examples:

- Defaulting to male pronouns for leadership roles
- Assuming all users are in the US
- Stereotypical examples (nurses are female, engineers are male)
- Cultural assumptions that don't fit Nordic context
- Demographic assumptions in examples

### Why it happens

AI is trained on internet text, which contains all the biases, stereotypes, and assumptions present in online content. It doesn't have intent or prejudice, but it reflects patterns in its training data.

Think of it like a mirror of the internet: it shows back what it was shown, including the problematic parts.

### Common bias patterns

**Gender bias in professional examples:**

- "The CEO met with his team..." (assuming male)
- "Ask your secretary to schedule..." (assuming female)
- "The engineer will test his code..." (assuming male)

**Geographic and cultural bias:**

- Assuming US context (dates, measurements, holidays)
- Examples that don't fit Nordic business culture
- Currency defaulting to USD or generic "dollars"

**Role stereotyping:**

- Nurses and teachers assumed female
- Executives and engineers assumed male
- Manual labor assumed less skilled

**Demographic assumptions:**

- Assuming all farmers are older males
- Assuming tech roles are young people
- Assuming family structures (married with children)

**Accessibility oversights:**

- Examples that assume everyone can see, hear, or move freely
- Interfaces designed only for mouse users
- Content that's not screen-reader friendly

### How to spot bias fast

**Read with fresh eyes:**

On your second read-through, specifically look for:

**Pronouns:**

Are "he/his" used for professional roles? Switch to "they/their" or rewrite.

**Examples:**

Do all the leaders have male names? Do all the assistants have female names?

**Geographic assumptions:**

Does it assume everyone is in the US or understands US references?

**Cultural fit:**

Would this language work in a Danish/Nordic business context?

**Inclusion:**

Could someone from a different background see themselves in these examples?

### What to do about bias

**Neutralize pronouns:**

Change "The manager should review his reports" to "Managers should review their reports" or "The manager should review the reports."

**Diversify examples:**

If AI gives you 5 examples with male names in leadership roles, change some names or rewrite to avoid gender entirely.

**Localize assumptions:**

Change US-centric examples to Nordic or European ones. Replace "Thanksgiving" with relevant local holidays.

**Check stereotypes:**

If you notice stereotypical patterns (all nurses female, all executives male), actively rewrite examples to break the pattern.

**Add context AI doesn't have:**

If AI doesn't know your actual customer demographics, adjust examples to match reality.

### Why this matters for Danish Agro

Your content represents the company. Biased content:

- Alienates employees and customers who don't fit stereotypes
- Looks unprofessional and outdated
- Can create legal or reputational issues
- Doesn't reflect Danish Agro's actual diverse workforce and customer base

Taking 30 seconds to check for bias makes your content better and more inclusive.

#### Mistake Type 4: Generic Output

### What it looks like

AI produces text that's grammatically perfect, reads smoothly, and says almost nothing useful. It's the corporate equivalent of empty calories.

Examples:

- Buzzwords without meaning ("synergy," "innovative solutions")
- Vague statements that could apply to any company
- Repetitive structure (every paragraph starts the same way)
- Lack of specific examples or concrete details
- "Professional" language that's actually just padding

### Why it happens

When AI doesn't have enough specific information about what you need, it falls back on common patterns it's seen many times. It generates "safe" corporate language that's technically correct but lacks substance.

Think of it like a student padding an essay: lots of words, not much actual content.

### Common generic patterns

**Empty buzzwords:**

- "Leveraging synergies to drive innovation"
- "Best-in-class solutions for optimal outcomes"
- "Cutting-edge technology to transform your business"
- "Streamlined processes for enhanced efficiency"

**Vague statements:**

- "We pride ourselves on excellent customer service"

   (What specifically makes it excellent?)

- "Our team of experts is ready to help"

   (Who? What expertise?)

- "We offer comprehensive solutions"

   (Comprehensive how? What solutions?)

**Repetitive structure:**

Every paragraph following the same pattern:

1. Generic statement
2. Vague supporting claim
3. Empty call to action

**Circular reasoning:**

- "Our product is innovative because it uses innovative technology"
- "We're the best choice because we deliver the best results"

**Hedging without reason:**

- "May," "might," "could" used excessively when you should be definitive
- "It's important to note that..." before obvious statements

### How to spot generic output fast

**The specificity test:**

Could this sentence apply to any company in your industry?

→ If yes, it's too generic

Could you replace "Danish Agro" with a competitor's name and it would still make sense?

→ If yes, add specific details

**The buzzword count:**

Count instances of:

- "Innovative," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class"
- "Synergy," "streamline," "leverage"
- "Solutions," "optimize," "transform"

More than 2-3 in a page of content? Time to edit.

**The "so what?" test:**

After each paragraph, ask "So what? What does this actually mean?"

If you can't answer with something specific and concrete, rewrite it.

### What to do about generic output

**Add specific details:**

Before: "We offer comprehensive agricultural solutions."

After: "We supply organic cattle feed, precision farming equipment, and agronomic consulting across Denmark and Sweden."

**Replace buzzwords with plain language:**

Before: "Leveraging our innovative platform to optimize your workflow."

After: "Our web portal lets you order supplies and track deliveries in real time."

**Use concrete examples:**

Before: "Our team has extensive expertise."

After: "Our agronomists have 15+ years of experience with dairy farming in Nordic climates."

**Cut the fluff:**

Before: "It is important to note that our customer service representatives are available to assist you."

After: "Contact customer service: 12 34 56 78"

**Make claims specific and verifiable:**

Before: "Most customers are satisfied."

After: "92% of customers rated our service 4 or 5 stars in our 2024 survey."

### Why generic content fails

**For readers:**

- Wastes their time
- Doesn't answer their actual questions
- Sounds like every other company
- Makes you seem less credible

**For you:**

- Doesn't showcase your actual value
- Doesn't help customers make decisions
- Doesn't differentiate Danish Agro
- Requires more editing to become useful

**Better to write less content that says something specific than more content that says nothing.**

# Common AI Mistakes

## Learn to spot the patterns before they cause problems

AI makes predictable mistakes. Once you know what to look for, you'll catch most errors in seconds. This page shows you the most common patterns and how to spot them fast.

---

## Why these patterns matter

AI doesn't make random errors. It makes **systematic** ones based on how it works. The same types of mistakes appear again and again across different tools and different tasks.

Learning these patterns is like learning to proofread: at first you read every word carefully, but eventually you start recognizing common typos instantly. This page gives you that pattern recognition for AI mistakes.

---

## The big four mistake categories

AI mistakes fall into four main types. Each has telltale signs you can learn to spot quickly.

**1. Hallucinations** - Making things up

AI invents facts, sources, statistics, and quotes with complete confidence

**2. Outdated information** - Wrong about timing

AI's knowledge has a cutoff date and doesn't know what happened after

**3. Bias and assumptions** - Unfair defaults

AI reflects biases from its training data and makes cultural assumptions

**4. Generic output** - Sounds good, says nothing

AI produces fluent text that's technically correct but lacks substance

---

## Quick reference: Spot the mistake

Use this quick guide when reviewing AI output:

**See specific statistics without sources?**

→ Probably hallucination. Verify or remove.

**See job titles, product names, or prices?**

→ Probably outdated. Check current information.

**See assumptions about gender, culture, or roles?**

→ Probably bias. Rewrite for neutrality.

**See lots of buzzwords and vague claims?**

→ Probably generic. Add specific details or cut it.

**See "recent" or "currently"?**

→ Check: recent when? Currently as of what date?

**See a quote from a real person?**

→ Verify they actually said it.

**See internal company data you didn't provide?**

→ Invented. Remove it.

**See perfect-sounding but unverifiable claims?**

→ Too good to be true. Check it.

---

## The two-minute mistake scan

When you're in a hurry, do this quick check:

**30 seconds: Statistics and sources**

Any numbers or citations? Verify the most important one.

**30 seconds: Names and roles**

Any people mentioned? Verify current titles and roles.

**30 seconds: Pronouns and bias**

Scan for "he/his" in professional contexts. Check stereotypes.

**30 seconds: Buzzword count**

More than 2-3 empty buzzwords? Flag for editing.

**If you found problems:** Do a deeper check with the full validation framework.

**If everything looks clean:** Good to go, but stay alert.

---

## Learning from mistakes

**When you catch an AI mistake:**

1. **Note the pattern:** What type was it? Hallucination? Bias? Generic?
2. **Check for more:** If you found one, there might be others of the same type
3. **Update your prompts:** Add specific instructions to avoid this mistake next time
4. **Share with colleagues:** Help your team learn to spot the same patterns

**Example:**

If AI invents statistics in one response, check all other claims with numbers. Then in your next prompt, add: "Do not include statistics unless you're certain they're accurate. If uncertain, say so."

---

## Common mistake combinations

AI mistakes often appear together. If you spot one, check for others:

**Hallucination + Outdated = Double trouble**

AI invents a statistic AND dates it to 2023, making it sound recent and verified.

**Generic + Bias = Vague stereotypes**

"Our team of expert engineers" (generic) + assuming all male (bias)

**Outdated + Hallucination = Confident nonsense**

Real person, old job title, invented quote

**When one mistake appears, slow down and check the rest carefully.**

---

## Building your mistake-spotting skills

**You'll get faster with practice:**

**Week 1:** Mistakes take 5 minutes to spot

**Week 4:** You see hallucinations immediately

**Week 8:** You catch bias and generic content while reading

**Week 12:** It's automatic - like spell-checking

**How to practice:**

**Daily:** Review one piece of AI output specifically looking for mistakes

**Weekly:** Compare before/after when you've edited AI content

**Monthly:** Notice which mistake types you catch most easily

**The goal isn't perfection - it's building pattern recognition.**

---

## When mistakes aren't mistakes

**Not every unusual output is wrong:**

**AI challenges your assumption:**

Sometimes AI's different perspective is actually valuable. If something surprises you, verify it before assuming it's wrong.

**AI uses unexpected phrasing:**

Different wording isn't necessarily wrong - just different from how you'd say it.

**AI is more formal/casual than you expected:**

That's a style choice, not an error (though you should adjust it to match your voice).

**Real mistakes have factual, logical, or ethical problems. Style preferences are just preferences.**

---

## Remember

AI makes predictable mistakes. Learn the patterns, and you'll catch them before they cause problems.

**The four big types:**

1. Hallucinations (making things up)
2. Outdated information (old facts)
3. Bias (unfair assumptions)
4. Generic output (empty language)

**The quick check:**

Statistics → verify

Names/roles → check current

Pronouns → neutralize

Buzzwords → cut

**The mindset:**

Trust but verify. AI is a tool, not a source of truth.

# Training & Field Documentation

How to turn photos from training sessions into structured, reusable materials using AI.

---

## The use case

During hands-on training sessions — such as CLAAS equipment training — trainers often explain procedures by drawing diagrams or writing on whiteboards. Participants take photos of these notes afterwards. AI can turn those photos into clean, structured training materials in minutes.

---

## How it works

1. Take a photo of the whiteboard, drawing, or handwritten notes during the session.
2. Upload the photo to an AI tool that supports image input (such as ChatGPT or Copilot).
3. Ask the AI to turn the content into structured training material.
4. Review, edit, and save the output.

---

## Example prompts

**For a step-by-step guide:** "Turn this whiteboard photo into a step-by-step guide for a new technician."

**For a checklist:** "Create a checklist based on what is written and drawn here."

**For structured notes:** "Summarise the key points from this training photo into clean, numbered notes."

---

## Tips for better results

- Take photos in good light with a straight angle — the clearer the image, the better the output.
- Be specific in your prompt. Tell the AI who the audience is and what format you need.
- Always review the output. AI can misread handwriting or miss context that was explained verbally.
- If the photo contains sensitive technical information or proprietary procedures, use Copilot rather than external tools.

---

> This is a strong example of AI handling unstructured, visual input and turning it into something reusable. The effort is low, the output is high — exactly the kind of task AI handles well.

#### From Raw Notes to First Draft

# From Raw Notes to First Draft

How to turn scattered product facts and rough notes into sharp first drafts for newsletters, social posts, product copy, and sales material.

---

## The use case

You have product facts, bullet points, and scattered notes. You need a polished first draft adapted to a specific audience, channel, and purpose. AI can handle the structure and drafting work so you can focus on the commercial angle, quality, and final judgment.

> The goal is to remove 60-80% of the groundwork, not to remove the person. AI does the draft. You make the final call.

---

## How it works

Give the AI three things and it will give you a usable first draft:

1. Raw input: paste in your product facts, notes, or bullet points directly. Do not describe them, just paste them.
2. Audience: who is this for? A customer, a retailer, a new employee?
3. Format and purpose: newsletter intro, LinkedIn post, product description, sales email?

---

## Prompt structure that works

Here are my raw notes: [paste notes]. Turn this into a [newsletter intro / LinkedIn post / product description / sales email] for [audience]. Tone: [direct / warm / commercial / informative]. Keep it under [word count]. I will edit the final version myself.

---

## Examples by format

Newsletter intro: "Here are my notes on our new feed product launch. Turn this into a 100-word newsletter intro for Danish livestock farmers. Tone: direct and informative."

LinkedIn post: "Here are the key facts about our sustainability initiative. Turn this into a LinkedIn post for a professional audience. Keep it under 150 words and end with a question."

Product description: "Here are the technical specs and benefits of this product. Write a product description for our website aimed at farmers with mid-size operations. Focus on practical benefits, not features."

Sales email: "Here are my notes from the customer meeting. Draft a follow-up sales email based on what we discussed. Tone: warm and confident."

---

## Tips for better results

- Paste raw input directly rather than describing it. Direct input consistently produces better output.
- If you work with the same product categories or audiences regularly, save a prompt template with your tone of voice and audience description. It saves time across all future drafts.
- AI is good at structure and flow. It does not know your commercial angle or strategic priorities. That is your job.
- Always review and edit the output. The draft is a starting point, not a finished product.
- For content involving customer data or internal product information, use Copilot rather than external tools.

**Why it’s okay:** Anonymized, no personal data, generic scenario.

- If you can remove all specific numbers and only ask for structuring: MAYBE
