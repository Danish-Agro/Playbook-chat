# Tools

## Understanding the AI tool landscape at Danish Agro

**Microsoft Copilot is our recommended tool** — but it's not the only AI tool out there. This page helps you understand what tools exist, what they're good for, and how to choose safely.

If you've used AI at work, you've probably tried ChatGPT, Claude, or other tools. You're not alone — our data shows 60 different AI tools in use across Danish Agro.

**That's not a problem.** Different tasks sometimes need different tools.

## The broader AI tool landscape

Beyond Copilot, these are the AI tools you're most likely to encounter:

### Text & Conversation AI

**ChatGPT (by OpenAI)**

- **What it is:** The most well-known AI chatbot. General-purpose text generation and conversation.
- **Strengths:** Versatile, good at most tasks, large user community means lots of tips and examples.
- **Limitations:** Free version trains on your data (you can opt out). Not connected to your work systems.
- **Best for:** General writing, brainstorming, learning, personal productivity.
- **When to use:** Non-sensitive tasks that don't involve company data.

**Claude (by Anthropic)**

- **What it is:** Advanced AI assistant focused on long-form content and analysis.
- **Strengths:** Exceptional at handling very long documents (up to 500 pages). Strong reasoning and analysis.
- **Limitations:** No integration with your work systems. Still trains on data unless you opt out.
- **Best for:** Analyzing long reports, complex reasoning tasks, code review.
- **When to use:** When you need to work with very long documents and the content is non-sensitive.

**Google Gemini**

- **What it is:** Google's AI assistant, integrated with Google Workspace.
- **Strengths:** Works with Google Docs, Gmail, Sheets. Good at research and information gathering.
- **Limitations:** Google ecosystem only. Privacy considerations with Google's data policies.
- **Best for:** If you work heavily in Google Workspace (which Danish Agro doesn't use as primary system).
- **When to use:** Personal productivity in Google tools, research tasks.

**Le Chat (by Mistral)**

- **What it is:** European-based AI assistant with strong multilingual support.
- **Strengths:** EU-based (data residency), excellent for European languages, good at code.
- **Limitations:** Smaller user base, less integration with business tools.
- **Best for:** Multilingual content, users who prefer EU-based AI providers.
- **When to use:** Translation work, multilingual content creation.

### Specialized AI Tools

Beyond general chatbots, you'll encounter AI built into other tools:

**Image Generation**

- DALL-E, Midjourney, Adobe Firefly, Stable Diffusion
- **What they do:** Generate images from text descriptions, edit photos, create variations.
- **When you might use them:** Marketing materials, presentations, visual brainstorming.
- **Note:** Be aware of copyright and usage rights. AI-generated images can't always be used commercially.

**Audio & Voice**

- ElevenLabs, Murf, Descript, [Otter.ai](http://Otter.ai)
- **What they do:** Voice generation, transcription, audio editing.
- **When you might use them:** Podcast production, meeting transcriptions, voiceovers.
- **Note:** Voice cloning raises ethical concerns. Always disclose if audio is AI-generated.

**Video**

- Runway, Synthesia, Descript
- **What they do:** Generate video clips, AI avatars, video editing.
- **When you might use them:** Training videos, presentations, content creation.
- **Note:** Emerging technology. Quality varies, ethical considerations apply.

**Code Assistants**

- GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Replit AI
- **What they do:** Code generation, debugging, code completion.
- **When you might use them:** Software development, scripting, automation.
- **Note:** Always review generated code for security and correctness.

**Reading & Transcription**

- ReadSpeaker, Speechify, [Otter.ai](http://Otter.ai)
- **What they do:** Text-to-speech, speech-to-text, accessibility support.
- **When you might use them:** Accessibility, meeting notes, content consumption.

**Writing Assistants**

- Grammarly, Jasper, [Copy.ai](http://Copy.ai)
- **What they do:** Grammar checking, content generation, copywriting.
- **When you might use them:** Proofreading, marketing copy, content creation.
- **Note:** Many of these now use AI features. Check their privacy policies.

## When it's okay to use tools other than Copilot

**Green light 🟢 - Go ahead:**

- **Personal learning and development**

"Help me understand how supply chain optimization works"

- **General brainstorming with no company specifics**

"Give me 10 creative campaign ideas for promoting sustainable farming"

- **Public information research**

"Explain the EU's new agricultural regulations"

- **Draft content that you'll heavily edit**

"Write a first draft of a blog post about [public topic]"

- **Learning to use AI itself**

Experimenting with different tools to understand their strengths

**Yellow light 🟡 - Proceed with caution:**

- **Working with anonymized data**

Remove all names, numbers, and identifying details first

- **Templates and structures**

"Create an outline for a customer presentation" (with no specific customer details)

- **General work scenarios**

"How should I structure a project kickoff meeting?" (without revealing project details)

**Red light 🔴 - Use Copilot instead:**

- **Any customer information**

Names, contact details, purchase history, communications

- **Internal company data**

Financial figures, strategy documents, pricing, roadmaps

- **Colleague information**

Performance data, salary info, personal details

- **Unpublished information**

Product launches, M&A activity, confidential projects

- **Anything you wouldn't put on a public drive**

## Choosing the right tool for your task

### For most work tasks → **Microsoft Copilot**

Use Copilot as your default when:

- You're working with company information
- You need AI integrated with Word, Outlook, Teams, Excel
- You want to search across your work files and emails
- The task is part of your daily job responsibilities

---

### For analyzing very long documents → **Claude**

Consider Claude when:

- You have a 50+ page report to analyze
- You need to work with multiple long documents simultaneously
- The content is non-sensitive (or properly anonymized)
- You need deep analysis and reasoning

**Then:** Use Copilot if the document is internal/sensitive

---

### For general learning and experimentation → **ChatGPT**

Use ChatGPT when:

- You're learning about AI and want to experiment
- The task involves only public information
- You want to try different prompting techniques
- You're working on personal projects

**Remember:** Turn off training in privacy settings

---

### For multilingual content → **Le Chat** or **Gemini**

Consider these when:

- You need strong support for European languages
- You're translating between Danish, Swedish, German, etc.
- You prefer EU-based AI providers
- The content is non-sensitive

---

### For creative visuals → **Image generation tools**

Use DALL-E, Midjourney, etc. when:

- You need visual inspiration or mockups
- You're creating presentation graphics
- You want to explore visual concepts
- You understand copyright limitations

**Note:** Always check usage rights before using AI images commercially

---

### For code and development → **GitHub Copilot** or **Claude**

Use specialized code tools when:

- You're writing or debugging code
- You need help with technical implementation
- You're learning a new programming language
- The code doesn't contain proprietary business logic

## Safety rules that apply to ALL tools

Regardless of which AI tool you use, these rules always apply:

**1. Never share confidential data**

Customer info, internal financials, sensitive strategy, personal data about colleagues.

**2. Turn off training in privacy settings**

Most tools use your conversations to improve their models unless you opt out.

**3. Always review and edit output**

AI makes mistakes. Check facts, verify sources, add your voice.

**4. Be transparent about AI use**

Don't pretend AI-generated content is entirely your own original work.

**5. When in doubt, ask**

Not sure if something is safe? Ask before sharing.

**See the Golden Rules for complete guidance.**

## Tips for using any AI tool safely

**1. Start with public information**

Test tools with non-sensitive content first. Build comfort before using them for work.

**2. Anonymize when possible**

Replace names with "Customer A," remove specific numbers, generalize details.

**3. Review privacy policies**

Understand what each tool does with your data. Look for opt-out options.

**4. Keep work and personal separate**

Use work account for Copilot, personal account for experimenting with other tools.

**5. Document what works**

Share successful prompts and approaches with colleagues. We learn together.
