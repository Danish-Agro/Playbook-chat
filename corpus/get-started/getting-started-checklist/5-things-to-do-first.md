## Your quick start guide to AI at work

Before you start using AI for real tasks, take 15 minutes to set yourself up properly. These five steps will protect your data, improve your results, and give you confidence that you're doing things right.

---

## The quick version

1. **Choose your tool.** Start with Microsoft Copilot. It's integrated with your work account and has enterprise security built in.
2. **Configure your privacy settings.** Turn off data sharing for training. This stops your conversations from being used to improve AI models.
3. **Set up custom instructions.** Tell the AI who you are and how you like responses. You'll get better, more relevant answers.
4. **Read the Golden Rules.** Five principles that keep you and the company safe. Takes 3 minutes.
5. **Run your first test.** Try a simple, low stakes task. See how AI responds before you use it for anything important.

## The detailed version

### 1. Choose your tool

**What to do**

Start with Microsoft Copilot. Open it at [copilot.microsoft.com](http://copilot.microsoft.com) or through your Microsoft 365 apps.

**Why Copilot?**

Copilot is Danish Agro's recommended AI tool. When you're signed in with your work account, your data stays within our Microsoft environment. It's not used to train AI models, and it's covered by our enterprise agreements.

Other tools like ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini are fine for non-sensitive tasks, but Copilot should be your default for anything involving company information.

### 2. Configure your privacy settings

**What to do**

Open your AI tool's settings and look for privacy or data controls. Turn off any option that allows your conversations to be used for training or model improvement.

**Why it matters**

By default, many AI tools learn from your conversations. That means your prompts, your questions, and any data you include could be reviewed by employees at the AI company or used to shape how the model responds to others.

For work use, you want to avoid this. Turning off training takes 30 seconds and ensures your conversations stay private.

**Where to find it**

Each tool is different. See "Configuring your AI tool" for step by step instructions for ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, Gemini, and Le Chat.

### 3. Set up custom instructions

**What to do**

Most AI tools let you create a profile or set of instructions that apply to all your conversations. Use this to tell the AI about your role, your company, and how you prefer to receive information.

**Example**

"I work in marketing at Danish Agro, an agricultural company. I create content for farmers and agricultural professionals. I prefer clear, direct language without corporate jargon. Give me practical suggestions I can use immediately."

**Why it matters**

Without context, AI gives generic answers. With context, it gives relevant ones. Custom instructions mean you don't have to explain who you are and what you need in every single conversation.

This isn't just about convenience. It genuinely improves the quality of what you get back.

**Where to find it**

See "Configuring your AI tool" for instructions specific to your tool.

### 4. Read the Golden Rules

**What to do**

Read through the five Golden Rules. They're the foundation for safe, responsible AI use at Danish Agro.

**The rules**

1. Always review AI output
2. Never share confidential data
3. Be transparent when you use AI
4. Use AI to enhance, not replace, your expertise
5. When in doubt, ask

**Why it matters**

These rules aren't bureaucratic box ticking. They exist because AI makes mistakes, data protection matters, and your professional judgment is irreplaceable.

Understanding the rules helps you use AI with confidence. You'll know what's allowed, what isn't, and what to do when you're unsure.

**Go deeper**

Each rule has its own page with examples and practical guidance. See "Golden Rules" for the full details.

### 5. Run your first test

**What to do**

Before you use AI for anything important, try it on something simple and low stakes. Ask it to help you brainstorm ideas, summarise a public article, or draft an email you weren't going to send anyway.

**Why it matters**

AI tools behave differently than you might expect. They're confident even when wrong. They sometimes miss the point. They need clear instructions to give good results.

A test run helps you understand how the tool works without any pressure. You'll learn what kinds of prompts work well, how to refine your requests, and what to watch out for.

**What to try**

- "Give me five headline ideas for a blog post about [topic]"
- "Summarise this article in three bullet points: [paste public article]"
- "Help me brainstorm ways to improve [process or project]"

Start simple. See what happens. Adjust and try again.
