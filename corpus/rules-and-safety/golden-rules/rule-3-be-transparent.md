## Rule 3: Be transparent when you use AI

**Honesty builds trust — with customers, colleagues and yourself.**

### Why this matters

There's nothing wrong with using AI to help with your work. But people deserve to know when they're reading AI-assisted content, especially customers and stakeholders.

Transparency isn't about apologizing for using AI. It's about being professional and maintaining trust. It shows you're using AI responsibly, not trying to deceive anyone.

### What this means in practice

**When you should disclose AI use:**

✅ **Always disclose when:**

- Sending AI-generated content to customers or external partners
- Publishing content on our website, social media, or marketing materials
- Sharing reports or analysis with leadership
- Creating official documents or communications
- Using AI for customer service responses

✅ **Consider disclosing when:**

- Collaborating with colleagues on projects
- Presenting work in meetings
- Sharing drafts for feedback

❌ **You don't need to disclose when:**

- Using AI for personal brainstorming or idea generation
- Getting help with grammar/spell checking
- Using AI as a research assistant (but verify facts!)
- Asking AI for suggestions you completely rewrite

**The key question:** Will someone else see or be affected by this content? If yes, be transparent.

### Real example

**Situation:** You've used AI to draft a blog post for Danish Agro's website about sustainable farming practices.

**✅ Good disclosure options:**

**Option 1 (At the end of article):**

> *This article was created with AI assistance and reviewed by our agricultural experts to ensure accuracy and relevance.*

**Option 2 (In byline):**

> *By Jonas Frank, with AI-assisted research and drafting*

**Option 3 (Subtle but clear):**

> *Note: We use AI tools to help research and structure our content, with final review and editing by our team.*

**Option 4 (Internal document header):**

> *Draft created with ChatGPT | Reviewed and edited by Jonas Frank | 6 Dec 2024*

### Different situations, different approaches

**Customer emails:**

```other
Hi [Customer],

Here's a summary of our meeting (drafted with AI assistance from my notes).

Please let me know if I've missed anything or if you need clarification.

Best,
Jonas
```

**Internal reports:**

```other
Q4 Marketing Report
Prepared by: Jonas Frank
Research assistance: Claude AI
Final review: Marketing team
```

**Social media:**

```other
[Post content]

#SustainableFarming #DanishAgro #AIAssisted
```

### Quick checklist

- [ ] Have I clearly stated that AI was used?
- [ ] Does my disclosure match the formality of the content?
- [ ] Am I being honest about how much AI contributed?
- [ ] Would I be comfortable if someone asked "Did you use AI for this?"
- [ ] Have I verified that AI-generated facts are accurate?

### When you're not sure

If you're uncertain whether to disclose:

- **Default to transparency** — it's always the safer choice
- **Ask yourself:** "Would I want to know if I was reading this?"
- **Consider the context** — customer-facing content needs more transparency than internal brainstorming
- **Check with your manager** if it's a high-stakes communication

### Why this matters for you

Being transparent about AI use:

- ✅ Builds trust with your audience
- ✅ Shows you're using technology responsibly
- ✅ Protects you if something goes wrong
- ✅ Sets a good example for others
- ✅ Aligns with our values of honesty and integrity

**Remember:** Using AI is smart. Hiding it is risky.

## Why we have this rule

AI is becoming a normal part of professional work. Being open about it shows we're leading with integrity, not cutting corners. It's about respect — for our customers, our colleagues, and our professional standards.
