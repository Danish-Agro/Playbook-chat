## Rule 5: When in doubt, ask

**Questions are encouraged — uncertainty alone is risky.**

### Why this matters

The biggest risk with AI isn't using it wrong — it's *not asking* when you're unsure.

Every single person using AI has questions. The difference between those who succeed and those who struggle isn't knowledge — it's willingness to ask.

We'd much rather you ask a "simple" question than worry in silence or make a mistake because you were afraid to speak up.

### What this means in practice

**There are no stupid questions about AI. Only unasked ones.**

✅ **Always OK to ask about:**

- "Can I use AI for this specific task?"
- "Is this information safe to share with AI?"
- "How do I know if this output is good enough?"
- "Which tool should I use for this?"
- "Did I do something wrong?"
- "I'm not sure I understand how this works"
- "Everyone else seems to know this, but I don't"

✅ **Who you can ask:**

- **Your manager** — they want you to succeed
- **AI Playbook team** — [jfra@danishagro.dk](mailto:jfra@danishagro.dk)
- **IT Support** — for technical questions
- **Data Protection Officer** — for data security questions
- **Colleagues** — they probably have the same questions

**No question is too basic. No concern is too small.**

### Real example

**Situation:** You want to use AI to help with a customer proposal, but you're not sure what's safe to share.

**❌ What NOT to do:**

- Guess and hope for the best
- Avoid using AI entirely (and fall behind)
- Copy what you saw someone else do without understanding
- Keep worrying but never ask

**✅ What TO do:**

**Quick question to your manager:**

> "Hey, I want to use AI to help structure a proposal for [customer]. I won't share any confidential pricing or terms — just use it for outline and structure. Does that sound OK?"

**Or to AI Playbook team:**

> "I'm working on [type of project] and wondering: what's safe to share with AI and what should I keep confidential? Can we do a quick 10-min call?"

**Result:** You get clarity, use AI confidently, and learn for next time.

### Common concerns (and why they're OK)

**"Everyone else seems to know this already"**

→ They don't. They're either figuring it out as they go, or they asked someone else.

**"I'll look incompetent if I ask"**

→ The opposite is true. Asking shows you care about doing things right.

**"I should have learned this by now"**

→ AI is evolving rapidly. No one has it all figured out.

**"What if my question is really obvious?"**

→ If you're asking, others are wondering too.

**"I don't want to bother people"**

→ Helping colleagues with AI is literally part of the Playbook team's job.

### Quick checklist

Ask for help when:

- [ ] You're not sure if something is confidential
- [ ] You've tried something and it's not working
- [ ] The AI output seems wrong but you don't know how
- [ ] You need to make a decision and feel uncertain
- [ ] You've been stuck on something for more than 15 minutes
- [ ] You think "Am I allowed to do this?"

**If any of these are true: Stop and ask. Right now.**

### How to ask effectively

**Good question structure:**

1. **Context:** "I'm working on [project/task]"
2. **Specific situation:** "I want to use AI for [specific purpose]"
3. **Your question:** "Is it OK to [specific action]?" or "How should I [specific task]?"
4. **What you've tried (if applicable):** "I tried [X] but [result]"

**Example:**

> "I'm drafting a product description for our website. I want to use ChatGPT to generate 3 different versions, then choose the best one and edit it. I won't share any pricing or customer data. Does that sound like a good approach?"

**This is clear, specific, and easy for someone to answer quickly.**

### When you're not sure

If you're uncertain whether your question is "worth asking":

- **Ask anyway** — seriously, just ask
- **Remember:** The Playbook exists because people have questions
- **Think about it this way:** Would you want a colleague to ask you? Of course you would.

## Why we have this rule

We found that many people use AI but hesitate to share their experiences — even when things go wrong. That stops everyone from learning.

By making questions normal and encouraged, we create an environment where everyone can use AI confidently and improve together.

**Remember:** You're not doing anything wrong — we're learning together.
